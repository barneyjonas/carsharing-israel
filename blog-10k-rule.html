Why carsharing makes financial sense (if you drive under 10,000 km/year)

The European benchmark that also applies in Israel — and how to calculate it for yourself.

If you drive under 10,000 km a year, carsharing is likely cheaper than owning a car. This is not just a European rule — it applies in Israel too.

The fixed costs of car ownership

Owning a car in Israel means paying every month whether you drive or not:

  • Insurance: ₪800–1,500/month depending on the driver and vehicle
  • Parking: ₪400–800/month in Tel Aviv and central cities
  • Fuel: ₪300–600/month for average urban driving
  • Maintenance & unexpected repairs: ₪200–400/month averaged

Total: roughly ₪1,700–3,300/month — before the car payment itself.

What carsharing costs instead

With carsharing, you pay only for what you use. A typical month with occasional trips might look like:

  • Monthly membership (if required): ₪0–200
  • 3–4 trips per week × ~₪30–60 each: ₪400–900

Total: roughly ₪400–1,100/month — a significant saving if you do not depend on a car daily.

The 10,000 km rule

In Austria and Germany, the carsharing industry uses 10,000 km/year as the break-even threshold. Below that, the per-trip costs of carsharing are almost always lower than the fixed costs of ownership. Israel's higher parking costs and urban density make the threshold even more favourable for carsharing.

How to calculate it for yourself

  1. Track how many kilometres you actually drove last year
  2. Add up your monthly fixed car costs (insurance + parking + maintenance)
  3. Estimate your monthly carsharing cost based on your trip frequency
  4. Compare the two numbers

If you drive under 10,000 km/year and live in or near a city with carsharing coverage, the numbers almost always favour carsharing.

Note. Costs are approximate and vary by provider, city, and driver profile. Always compare actual pricing with your specific provider.
